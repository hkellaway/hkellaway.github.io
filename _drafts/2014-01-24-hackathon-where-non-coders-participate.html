Is there such a thing?

I'm here to tell you: there is! I recently attended a hackathon where non-coders were an important part of the picture. I may not have personally taken note of the wealth in attendance, except I sensed an uneasiness among the attendees who didn't code when we started off with an ice-breaker: tell us your name, tell us what you do, tell us why you're here.

This was juxtaposed against the fact that our very first two presenters -- Janet Mock and Sarah Prager -- are distinctly not programmers themselves. How did two non-programmers end up headlining the first hour of a hackathon? How did the first place team end up with non-coders? How did the third place project not have a coder at all??

 

Everyone has something to bring to the table when a diverse pool of people are to receive the benefits of the hacks produced -- coders do not have monopolies on good ideas or the ability to think of all the ideas or to do all of the things. Granted, it takes a certain kind of hackathon to be able to incorporate non-coders as meaningfully as coders.

Also, a broad interpretation of 'hack'.

Trans*H4CK chose the right style.

I'm taking a broad interpretation of the word 'hack' -- as in, hack something pertinent together in a short period of time.

Steal these ideas

- create new hashtag - #girlslikeus (#guyslikeus, #folkslikeus, make sure its unique)
- bomb hashtags or versions of hastags (e.g. #MakeoverMondays http://www.slate.com/blogs/future_tense/2013/11/20/manicuremonday_seventeen_magazine_s_hashtag_hijacked_by_scientists.html, http://www.bilerico.com/2014/01/lgbt_advocates_hijack_mcdonalds_cheerstosochi_camp.php)

- 'social media domino' - Tumblr > Twitter > Facebook/Paper.li
